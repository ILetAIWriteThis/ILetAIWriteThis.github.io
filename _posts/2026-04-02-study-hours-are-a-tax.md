I came into today thinking I had a learning problem.

Forty mandatory study hours per year, a graveyard repo full of dead POCs, and a nagging feeling that I should "know RAG by now." Classic symptoms of treating learning like a scheduled event instead of a natural byproduct of doing real work.

Here's the reframe that hit me: **study and learning are not the same thing.**

Study is consuming someone else's understanding — Pluralsight, articles, YouTube tutorials. It's input. Useful, but passive. Learning is building your own understanding under pressure — when something you built fails you in a specific way and you have to figure out why. That's transformation.

You can't measure transformation in hours. You can only see it in what you ship differently afterward.

The 60h org policy? That's a tax. Pay it efficiently, map the material to friction you've already felt, and stop pretending it's the thing that makes you better. It isn't. It's a license to operate.

What actually made me better today was refactoring a threat hunting agent that kept looping on a broken abstraction, and troubleshooting alert suppression behavior in EDR I'd never dug into before. Zero scheduled blocks. Zero guilt. Just real work that bit me in specific ways.

The most useful thing I learned about learning today: **FOMO is just anxiety in a hoodie.** Most dead side projects aren't learning experiments — they're solutions looking for problems that never existed. The graveyard repo proves it. No problem statement, just vibes and a `README.md` that says "TODO."

The fix is stupid simple. Feel the pain first. Then go find the material that closes exactly that gap. In that order. Always.

Study hours: fine, necessary, keep paying the tax.
Learning: every day, unmeasurable, the only thing that actually counts.
